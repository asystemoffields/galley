# The Harbor

It began, as these things do, with a *letter* no one would admit to sending.

Maren read it twice and burned it anyway. The fire took the paper the way the sea takes everything: politely at first, then all at once.

***

By morning the tide had carried off the ashes, which everyone agreed was **very dramatic** of it. The harbor went about its business. Gulls argued over rights to the fish heads. Old Brandt mended the same net he had been mending for eleven years, possibly the same hole.

Nobody mentioned the letter. That was how you knew the whole town had read it.

# The Crossing

The boat was named *Forgiveness*, which struck Maren as optimistic.

"She doesn't leak," said the ferryman, in the tone of a man who had recently finished bailing. "Much."

The crossing took six hours when the weather was kind. The weather, Maren would later write in her journal, *was not kind, has never been kind, and appears to hold a personal grudge.*

***

Halfway across, the fog came down like a curtain at the end of an act nobody had applauded.

# The Other Shore

There is a particular silence that belongs to arriving somewhere you swore you would never return to. The dock at Skeld had kept it warm for her, all these years.

Her mother's house still stood at the top of the salt road. The roof had new shingles. The door had a new lock.

The key in Maren's pocket, of course, was old.
